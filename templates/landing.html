About the No-Show Risk Predictor

This tool uses evidence-based predictors to assess the risk of patient no-shows at medical appointments. Understanding these factors helps healthcare providers implement targeted interventions to improve appointment attendance and optimize resource allocation.

Predictors and Their Justification

1. Prior No-Shows

Prior no-show history is consistently identified as the strongest predictor of future missed appointments, reflecting persistent behavioral patterns in healthcare utilization.

Justification: Assigned the highest weight due to strong and consistent predictive power across studies.

2. Lead Time (Days Between Scheduling and Appointment)

Longer lead times are associated with higher no-show rates, likely due to reduced patient commitment and increased likelihood of schedule conflicts.

Justification: Weighted highly as a key scheduling-related factor consistently shown to influence attendance.

3. Insurance Type (Private / Public / Uninsured)

Insurance status serves as a proxy for socioeconomic factors and access to care, with publicly insured or uninsured patients exhibiting higher no-show rates.

Justification: Included as a moderate predictor reflecting structural and financial barriers to care.

4. Age Group (18–34 / 35–64 / 65+)

Younger patients are more likely to miss appointments, while older adults tend to have higher adherence to scheduled care.

Justification: Assigned a lower weight as a demographic factor that influences behavior but is less predictive than prior attendance history.

5. Distance from Clinic (Near / Moderate / Far)

Greater distance from the clinic is associated with higher no-show rates due to transportation barriers and reduced accessibility.

Justification: Included as a system-level access factor representing logistical barriers to attending appointments.

📊 6. Appointment Time (Morning / Afternoon / Evening)

Appointment time has been identified as a system-level scheduling factor that can influence no-show rates, with variations attributed to work schedules, daily routines, and competing obligations.

Justification: Included as a secondary predictor to capture scheduling-related variability. Time of day is commonly used in predictive models but is generally less influential than behavioral and access-related factors.
